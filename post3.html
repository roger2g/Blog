SOURCE OF LIFE: THE IMPORTANCE OF OCEANS IN THE ENVIRONMENT

SOURCE OF LIFE

We live on land, but on a Planet full of water. The oceans are our huge blue heart and play a pivotal role in the environment and in the future of humanity.

Let's think, just for the sake of argument, that you've spent the last few decades living under a rock and that you think the oceans are pieces of water without value, interest or relevance. Try now to form a mental image of the Earth without them. How did you get out? An arid, greyish-white ball, reminiscent of… Mars? Then.

The oceans cover more than 70% of the Earth's surface and contain 97% of all the water on the planet. They influence climate and weather, stabilize temperature, shape terrestrial chemistry and provide a home for the greatest diversity of species on the planet. Every molecule of oxygen we breathe, every drop of water we drink, every food we eat, connects us to the ocean, whether we live on the coast or in the most remote and inhospitable inland part of the globe.

It is thought that the first forms of life developed in the ocean, still in the absence of oxygen, and that most of the oxygen in the atmosphere originally resulted from the actions of photosynthetic organisms in the ocean. In other words, it was the ocean that allowed, and it is still the ocean that allows, for the Earth to be habitable. As the American oceanographer Sylvia Earle, the world's greatest reference in the oceans and marine life, usually summarizes, “without blue, there is no green”. Because the key to the existence of life is water, and water is 97% oceans.

"MY FAULT"

It took about 4.5 billion years for the Earth to meet all the conditions to host us. But it took us just 4.5 decades to consume, deplete and significantly alter their highly delicate systems essential to our very survival.

The second half of the 20th century was dramatic for the “assets” of the planet. The ocean, which is the Earth's circulatory system, is clogged with hundreds of millions of tons of plastic and other waste, which, in conjunction with industrial fishing, removes hundreds of millions of wild animals from the scene, damaging the food chains that they shape the chemistry of the planet and influence the carbon cycle, the nitrogen cycle, the oxygen cycle, the water cycle, in other words, our life support system.

In this period, we lost – or rather, we took and consumed – more than 90% of large marine species, such as tuna and swordfish. The coral reefs are disappearing. And the health of the ocean suffers, with direct consequences for the health of the planet and, of course, for humanity.

ABSORB HEAT AND CO2

The oceans exert fundamental control over climate and weather conditions. They transport energy and dominate the water and carbon cycles, attenuating temperature fluctuations and maintaining the stability of the atmosphere's composition.

A big part of the oceans role is to absorb energy (heat), distributing it more evenly across the Earth. A few meters of the ocean's surface store as much heat as the entire Earth's atmosphere. So, as the planet warms, it is the ocean that receives most of the extra energy. And this has implications for the balance of ecosystems: when the ocean gets too hot, the life that inhabits it is forced to adapt… or die.

Fish and other marine animals breathe oxygen and release carbon dioxide, just like land animals. And marine plants absorb carbon dioxide and release oxygen, just like terrestrial plants (incidentally, seaweeds are responsible for producing much of the oxygen we breathe). But CO2 absorption is another vital function of the oceans, achieved through an ingenious “aspiration, mixing and retention” system.

The ocean absorbs carbon dioxide from the atmosphere whenever air meets water. Then the wind causes waves and turbulence, helping the CO2 to mix at the surface and penetrate deeper water, giving the water an opportunity to absorb carbon dioxide.

We owe the oceans to absorb about a quarter of the CO2 we emit. And as we don't stop emitting, the oceans are paying the price, seeing their chemistry changed and their water more acidic.

It's time to give back and embrace this cause. Because, going back to the wise words of oceanographer Sylvia Earle, “nothing else will matter if we fail to protect the oceans”.
