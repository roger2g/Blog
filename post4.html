FISH MAY BE THE ANSWER TO A MORE SUSTAINABLE WORLD

How does it help?

Replacing meat with certain types of sustainably sourced seafood can help people reduce their carbon footprints without compromising nutrition, according to an analysis of dozens of marine species that are consumed around the world.

A study, published Sept. 8 in Communications Earth & amp; Environment, suggests that farmed bivalves—molluscs such as mussels, clams, and oysters—and wild, small, and surface (pelagic) fish, which include anchovies, mackerel, and herring, generate fewer greenhouse gas emissions and are more nutrient-dense than than beef, pork or chicken.

“The research aimed to do a better job of understanding the climate impacts of seafood through the lens of very diverse nutritional qualities,” says co-author Peter Tyedmers, an ecological economist at Dalhousie University in Halifax, Canada.

The results echo previous studies, including work by members of Tyedmers' group that focused on seafood consumed in Sweden. "This time, the researchers wanted to include a more diverse global range of seafood," said the economist.

BENEFITS OF THE “BLUE” DIET

Food production is responsible for about a third of global greenhouse gas emissions, mainly methane and carbon dioxide. More than half are driven by livestock. Plant-based diets offer a lower-impact alternative to meat consumption, but "solutions tend to ignore the benefits of seafood-based diets," the study says.

Using 41 species of seafood, the researchers established a nutrient density score that accounted for essentials such as certain fats and vitamins. Species surveyed included fish, crustaceans, bivalves and cephalopods bred and caught in the wild (such as octopus and squid). The team then used available emissions data for 34 of these species to compare their nutrient density with emissions associated with their production or capture.

Half of the seafood species offered more nutritional benefits in terms of emissions. Pink salmon (Oncorhynchus gorbuscha), sockeye salmon (Oncorhynchus nerka) and small wild-caught pelagic fish, as well as farmed bivalves, were the best choices for nutrient-dense, low-emission protein sources. White fish such as cod (Gadus sp.) also had a low climate impact, but were among the least nutrient-dense foods. Wild-caught crustaceans had the highest emissions, with a carbon footprint rivaled only by beef. The authors note that their data do not include “post-production” emissions, such as those generated by refrigeration or transport.

The analysis adds more perspective to the role of seafood in food systems, says Zach Koehn, a marine scientist at the Stanford Center for Ocean Solutions in California. He adds that an obstacle in applying this research will be the need to make seafood more available and affordable, because those who could benefit from these nutrient-dense foods may not have access to them.

Tyedmers agrees that access to multiple diets is a privilege. “Every opportunity there is to replace seafood with beef is a small climate victory,” he says. "But it doesn't have to be every meal."
